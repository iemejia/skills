# Grammar and usage

Source: [Grammar](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/grammar/verbs) | [Acronyms](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/acronyms) | [Numbers](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/numbers)

## Verbs

### Tense

- Use **present tense** as the default. It's easier to read and understand.
- Avoid past and future tense unless describing something that genuinely happened or will happen at a specific time.

### Mood

| Mood | Use for | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Indicative** | Statements of fact, explanations — most content | "Style sheets are powerful tools for formatting complex documents." |
| **Imperative** | Instructions, procedures, direct commands | "Enter a file name, and then save the file." |
| **Subjunctive** | Avoid. Wishes, hypotheses. | ~~"We recommend that you be careful..."~~ |

Don't switch moods within a sentence.

### Active vs. passive voice

- **Default to active voice.** The subject performs the action.
- Use passive voice only when:
  - Avoiding blaming the customer in errors ("That site can't be found.")
  - Avoiding awkward constructions
  - Emphasizing the receiver of the action

| Avoid (passive) | Prefer (active) |
|---|---|
| The file is saved by the system. | The system saves the file. |
| The button should be clicked. | Select the button. |

### Verb agreement

- Group of things → singular verb: "A variety of games *is* available."
- Two things joined by *and* → plural verb: "Facebook and Instagram *are* available."
- Two things joined by *or* → match the closest subject.

## Nouns and pronouns

- Use second person (*you/your*) to address the reader.
- Use first person plural (*we/our*) when speaking as the organization.
- Avoid third person (*the user, one*) when you can use *you* instead.
- Make pronoun references unambiguous — the reader should always know what *it*, *this*, or *they* refers to.

## Acronyms

### Core rules

1. **Don't create acronyms from product or feature names.** Always spell out Microsoft product names.
2. **Only use acronyms your audience is familiar with.** Check Merriam-Webster or the A–Z word list.
3. **Spell out on first use** with the acronym in parentheses: "Conversation as a platform (CaaP)..."
4. **Don't introduce acronyms used only once.** Just spell out the term.
5. **Avoid acronyms in titles/headings** on first use unless needed for SEO.

### Formatting

- **Lowercase the spelled-out form** unless it's a proper noun: "dynamic-link library (DLL)" but "High-Definition Multimedia Interface (HDMI)."
- **Use *a* or *an* based on pronunciation**: "a URL" (you-are-ell), "an ISP" (eye-ess-pee).
- **Pluralize with lowercase *s***: "three APIs." Don't add *s* if the acronym already stands for a plural noun.
- **Avoid possessive form** unless referring to a person or organization.

## Numbers

### Numerals vs. words

- Spell out **zero through nine** in body text; use numerals for **10 or greater**.
- Always use numerals for: measurements, dimensions, temperatures, percentages, times, coordinates.
- If one item in a group requires a numeral, use numerals for all items of that type.
- Don't start a sentence with a numeral. Rewrite or spell it out.

### Formatting numbers

- **Commas**: use in numbers with four or more digits (1,024). Exception: years, pixels, baud with fewer than five digits (2500 B.C., 1920 × 1080 pixels).
- **Decimals**: add a zero before the decimal point for fractions less than one (0.5 cm).
- **Ranges**: use *from* and *through* ("from 9 through 17"). Use en dash in page ranges or tight spaces (pages 112–120).
- **Percentages**: numeral + *percent* (50 percent). Use *percentage* when unspecified.
- **Ordinals**: always spell out (the first row, the twenty-first anniversary). Don't use ordinals for dates.

### Dates

- Don't use ordinals for dates: use *June 1* not *June first*.
- Spell out the month to avoid ambiguity: *January 5, 2024* not *1/5/2024*.

### Phone numbers

- Use hyphens to separate parts: 612-555-0175.

### Abbreviations (K, M, B)

- Avoid abbreviating *thousand, million, billion*. Spell them out.
- If space requires it: capitalize (K, M, B), no space between number and letter (8M), avoid decimals with K.

## Person and point of view

- **Second person** (*you/your*) is the default for addressing readers. Omit *you can* when the sentence works without it.
- **First person singular** (*I, me, my*): use sparingly, only in product UI for user control (checkboxes, toggles). Never in marketing or support docs.
- **First person plural** (*we*): avoid when possible — it can feel corporate. OK for *we recommend* if it avoids passive, or in privacy/security where Microsoft is the speaker. Keep the focus on the customer.
- Don't mix points of view in a single section.
- For AI-generated content: use past tense ("for you") or words conveying uncertainty to indicate AI judgment.

## Nouns — proper vs. common

- Proper nouns (unique people, places, products, trademarks) are always capitalized.
- Common nouns (general categories: *cloud computing, smartphone, e-commerce*) are lowercase.
- Don't capitalize technology terms unless they're product names or industry-standard proper nouns.
- If unsure, check Merriam-Webster and the A–Z word list. Default to lowercase.

## Plural nouns

- Nouns ending in *s*: add *es* (biases, the Joneses).
- Singular abbreviations: add *s* (APIs, ISVs, DBMSs).
- Plural abbreviations: don't add *s* (MFC = Microsoft Foundation Classes).
- Single letters: add apostrophe + *s* with italic letter (*x*'s).
- Numbers: add *s* (the 1950s).
- Don't add *(s)* to indicate singular/plural — use the plural form.

## Collective nouns

- Collective nouns (*company, team*) take a singular pronoun: "The company upgraded *its* solution."
- Exception: if emphasis is on individuals in the group, plural is OK.

## Prepositions

### Prepositional phrases

- Avoid chaining more than two prepositional phrases — hard to read and easy to misinterpret.
- It's OK to end a sentence with a preposition when it improves readability.
