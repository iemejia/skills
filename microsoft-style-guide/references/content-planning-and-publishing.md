# Content planning and publishing

Source: [Content planning](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/content-planning) | [Responsive content](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/responsive-content) | [Search and writing](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/search-writing)

## Content planning

Great content starts with a plan. Ask these questions before writing:

1. **Who is your audience?** Define specifically — not just "developers" but what kind, what tools, what experience level.
2. **What do they want to accomplish?** Understand the task or problem driving them to your content.
3. **What is your business goal?** Every communication has a business reason.
4. **Do you have time or budget constraints?** Determines what content format is realistic.
5. **What format best meets customer needs?** Video, infographic, checklist, tutorial, article?
6. **How will the customer find it?** Plan for SEO, promotion, cross-links.
7. **Where will they look?** Documentation, community, blog, video platform?
8. **What devices will they use?** Plan for multi-device experiences.
9. **Will it be translated or localized?** Plan to streamline those processes.
10. **How will you measure success?** Define targets for views, engagement, conversions.

### Content types by user need

| Need | Content types |
|------|--------------|
| Simple task | UI text, documentation, short video, blog |
| Complex task | Video, tutorial, end-to-end scenario, technical paper, wizard |
| Timely updates | Blog, newsletter |
| Community answers | Wiki, forum |
| Comprehensive learning | Online training, tutorial, white paper, interactive graphic |
| Tips and tricks | UI text, microblogs, blogs, newsletters |
| Process understanding | Interactive flow chart, sample workflows |
| Troubleshooting | Interactive flow chart, UI troubleshooter |

## Search and writing (SEO)

### Keywords

- Identify search terms customers will use (not necessarily your internal terminology).
- Front-load keywords in: titles, headings, summaries, introductions, page descriptions, link text, alt text.
- Use keywords early and often without being contrived or repetitious.

### Where to place keywords

- Titles and headings
- Summaries and overviews
- Introductions and page descriptions
- Paragraph text (especially early)
- Link text
- Image and table alt text
- Video/audio text descriptions

### Links and backlinks

- Write short, descriptive link text (four or fewer words) including keywords.
- Use the target page's title or description as link text when it works.
- Link to background info rather than summarizing it.
- Seek backlinks from authoritative, relevant sites.
- Never create backlinks solely to improve search ranking.

## Responsive content

Assume content will be viewed on a variety of devices, including screens as small as 360 pixels wide.

### Keep it short

- Short text is always better, but on mobile it's imperative.
- Write sentences and paragraphs readable without scrolling on mobile.
- Keep headings to one short line when possible.
- Use short words in headings for accessibility (larger fonts may break long words across lines).
- Short sections are easier to read on small screens and to resume later.

### Simple visuals

- Choose simple images; crop extraneous detail.
- Add alt text to all meaningful images.
- Design infographics in compact, magnifiable sections.
- Simplify charts/graphs for small-screen readability.
- Limit table columns; keep cell text brief (ideally one line).

### Consider flow

- Think about how content reflows on narrow screens (right content moves below left).
- Work with designers to determine break points and content order at each resolution.

## Design planning

- Start writing early in the product development cycle.
- Collaborate with designers — writing and design inform each other.
- Write UI text concurrently with design, not after.
- Test content with users alongside design prototypes.

## Final publishing review

Before publishing, verify:

- [ ] Content answers the audience's key questions
- [ ] Voice and tone are consistent with Microsoft style
- [ ] Sentence-style capitalization is used throughout
- [ ] Oxford commas are present in all series
- [ ] No periods on headings or short list items
- [ ] Bias-free language throughout
- [ ] Alt text on all meaningful images
- [ ] Links are descriptive and working
- [ ] Keywords are front-loaded in headings and first paragraphs
- [ ] Content reads well on mobile/narrow screens
- [ ] Acronyms are spelled out on first use
- [ ] Numbers follow formatting guidelines
- [ ] Code examples compile and run correctly (if applicable)
- [ ] Accessibility requirements met (heading hierarchy, input-neutral verbs, no directional-only references)
