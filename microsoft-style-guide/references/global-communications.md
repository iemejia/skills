# Global communications

Source: [Global communications](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/global-communications/)

## Core principle

Assume your content will be read worldwide and may be translated into other languages or localized for specific markets.

## Key definitions

- **Translation**: changing the language of content (often automated via machine translation).
- **Localization**: adapting content (text, images, cultural references) to meet the language, cultural, and political expectations of a specific locale. Done by people familiar with local language and culture.

## Writing for global audiences

### Sentence structure

- Use simple, short sentences.
- Use standard subject-verb-object word order.
- Avoid complex subordinate clauses.
- Keep one idea per sentence.
- Avoid sentences longer than 25 words when possible.

### Vocabulary

- Use simple, common words. Avoid rare or obscure terms.
- Be consistent — use the same term for the same concept throughout.
- Avoid idioms, colloquialisms, and phrases that don't translate literally (*hit the ground running*, *low-hanging fruit*, *move the needle*).
- Avoid culturally specific references (sports analogies, holiday references, local food).
- Avoid humor that relies on wordplay or cultural context.

### Grammar for translation

- Use articles (*a, an, the*) to clarify meaning.
- Make pronoun references unambiguous — the reader should always know what *it*, *this*, or *they* refers to.
- Avoid noun strings (chains of three or more nouns used as modifiers). Rewrite with prepositions.
  - Avoid: "the database table migration script configuration"
  - Better: "the configuration for the database table migration script"
- Use relative pronouns (*that, which, who*) to clarify sentence structure.

### Numbers, dates, and measurements

- Don't assume date formats. If ambiguous, spell out the month (*January 5, 2024* not *1/5/2024*).
- Be explicit about time zones when referencing times.
- Use metric when possible, or provide both metric and imperial.
- Don't assume currency. Specify currency codes when needed (*USD, EUR*).

### Cultural sensitivity

- Don't make generalizations about people, countries, regions, or cultures — even positive or neutral ones.
- Don't assume a Western or US-centric perspective.
- Use examples that resonate across cultures.
- Verify that named geographic locations aren't politically disputed.
- Use equivalent geographic references (don't mix countries with states or continents in a single list).
